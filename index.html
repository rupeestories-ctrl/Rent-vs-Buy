How This Is Calculated
Click to expand

Amortization. Month-exact principal/interest split, not averaged. Tax savings are estimated using a simplified itemized vs standard deduction comparison ($30K MFJ / $15K Single under current TCJA). Mortgage interest + SALT (capped at $10K federal limit) vs standard deduction — difference taxed at your marginal rate. This is a simplification; actual tax benefit varies with your full return.

Capital gains. 7% transaction cost on sale (roughly 5.5% realtor + 1.5% closing). §121 exclusion applied ($500K MFJ / $250K Single). Excess taxed at a flat 15% long-term capital gains rate for simplicity. Adjust mentally if you expect a higher rate. High earners subject to NIIT (3.8% surcharge) should add that to their marginal rate manually.

Fair comparison. Both sides get credit for saved money. Early years when buying costs more → renter invests the annual cost difference. Later years when P&I is locked and rent rises above it → buyer invests the difference. Both pools compound at your investment return. The renter's starting portfolio includes both the down payment and closing costs — cash they didn't have to deploy.

Visa & exit friction. Selecting a visa status presets two things: a default stay-probability and a default exit-friction percentage. H1B defaults to 50% stay / 3% friction, GC-pending to 70% / 1%, Green Card to 85% / 0.5%, Citizen to 95% / 0%. Friction is only applied to H1B and GC-pending (where a forced international exit is plausible); on Green Card and Citizen paths, exit is treated as a domestic move with standard selling costs already baked into the 7% transaction fee. Override either value if your personal situation differs — manually-edited values are preserved when you click other visa buttons.

Why rent often wins in today's market. Historical data: stocks return ~7–10% nominal, US homes appreciate ~3–4% nominal. When the stock-return advantage exceeds 4% AND rent is below ~0.75% of home value per month, the renter who invests the down payment and cost difference typically comes out ahead. The math shifts toward buying when: rent exceeds 0.8%+ of home value, property taxes are low, mortgage rates drop, or the horizon is very long (25+ years).

Not modeled: PMI under 20% down, refinancing, rental conversion income, homestead exemptions, insurance spikes, NIIT surtax, state-level capital gains taxes. These can shift results; they rarely flip the verdict entirely.

This is a planning tool, not financial advice. The decision is less about buy vs rent, and more about how big a bet you're taking.
