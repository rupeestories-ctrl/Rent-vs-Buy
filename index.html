How This Is Calculated
Click to expand

Amortization. Month-exact principal/interest split, not averaged. PMI auto-calculated when down payment is below 20%: charged at the PMI rate on the current mortgage balance each year, cancels automatically once the balance drops to 78% of the original home price (per the Homeowners Protection Act). Refinance is optional: if enabled, at the specified year the model takes the current mortgage balance and rebuilds amortization at the new rate and term. Refi closing costs are pulled from the buyer's investment savings, which is the honest opportunity-cost treatment.

Standard deduction. 2026 values: $32,200 MFJ, $16,100 Single. These were increased permanently under OBBBA (July 2025) and continue to inflation-adjust annually.

SALT cap. Default $40,400 under OBBBA for tax year 2026, applied to property tax plus state/local income tax combined. The cap increases 1% annually through 2029 ($40,804 in 2027, $41,212 in 2028, $41,624 in 2029) then reverts to $10K in 2030. Phases down for MAGI above $505K in 2026 (30 cents per dollar over), floored at $10K — effectively back to $10K above $605K MAGI. Override the field if you're in phase-out territory or modeling pre-2025 rules.

Itemized deduction benefit. Each year: mortgage interest plus PMI premium plus capped SALT is compared to the standard deduction. Only the excess is taxed at your marginal rate — that's the real benefit. PMI premium deductibility was reinstated under OBBBA for tax year 2026 onward (it had been allowed to lapse after 2021). In low-interest later years, the benefit often disappears entirely because the standard deduction dominates.

Capital gains on sale. 7% transaction cost on gross sale (realtor plus closing). §121 exclusion applied: $500K MFJ / $250K Single. The remaining taxable gain is multiplied by an effective LTCG rate that combines: a federal bracket chosen from your marginal rate (0% if marginal ≤ 12%, 15% if 22–32%, 20% if 35%+), NIIT of 3.8% if the checkbox is on, and your state capital gains rate. The computed rate shows in the Tax section so you can sanity-check it.

Owner cost realism. Annual owner cost sums P&I (or post-refi P&I), property tax on current home value, insurance inflated annually, maintenance at your specified rate, HOA inflated annually, PMI if applicable, a reserves line for lumpy repairs (roof, HVAC, sewer line, special assessments), minus tax savings. The reserves field defaults to 0.25% of home value but real outcomes vary a lot — a new build eats near zero; a 40-year-old house with an aging roof can run 1%+.

Fair comparison on the rent side. Both scenarios get credit for cash not spent. Early years when buying costs more, the renter invests the cost difference. Later years when P&I is locked and rent rises above ownership cost, the buyer invests the difference. Both pools compound at the investment return. The renter's starting portfolio includes the down payment and closing costs — cash they didn't deploy.

Visa and exit modeling. Selecting a visa status presets stay-probability and exit-friction percent. H1B defaults to 50% stay / 3% friction, GC-pending to 70% / 1%, Green Card to 85% / 0.5%, Citizen to 95% / 0%. Friction and flat exit costs are only applied to H1B and GC-pending (where a forced international exit is plausible); Green Card and Citizen exits are treated as domestic moves with the standard 7% transaction cost already baked into the base sale math. Override any value if your situation differs — manually-edited fields are preserved when you click other visa buttons.

Why rent often wins in today's market. Stocks return ~7–10% nominal, US homes appreciate ~3–4% nominal. When the stock advantage exceeds 4% AND rent is below ~0.75% of home value per month, the renter who invests the down payment and cost difference typically wins. The math shifts toward buying when rent exceeds 0.8% of home value, property taxes are low, mortgage rates drop (or you refi), or the horizon is very long.

Not modeled: rental conversion income if you keep the property, homestead exemptions, state income tax on rental income if you convert, deducibility quirks for high earners, AMT, insurance spikes from wildfire/flood zones, property tax reassessment triggers (California Prop 13 vs everywhere else), full FIRPTA mechanics (the friction % is a proxy for the cash-flow lag, not the actual withholding). Consult a cross-border CPA for any of these before making the call.

This is a planning tool, not financial advice. The decision is less about buy vs rent, and more about how big a bet you're taking.
